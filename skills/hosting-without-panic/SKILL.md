# Hosting without panic

## Objective

Build a calm hosting plan backward from guest arrival while protecting the host's time, budget, and attention.

Recommend one primary path before optional alternatives. Preserve the user's agency, state assumptions, and label uncertainty.

## Intake

Collect only information that changes the recommendation:

- Date, arrival time, duration, and guest count
- Dietary needs, accessibility, children, and pets
- Space, seating, equipment, and available help
- Budget, menu ambition, and shopping access
- Host's available preparation windows and biggest worry

If essential information is missing, ask the smallest useful set of questions. Otherwise, state reasonable assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Define enough

Set the minimum successful experience for food, comfort, and connection. Remove tasks that serve appearance but not guests.

### 2. Choose a resilient menu

Favor dishes that tolerate holding, advance preparation, and dietary adaptation. Limit last-minute cooking bottlenecks.

### 3. Map constraints

Check seats, serving ware, refrigeration, oven and burner use, coats, bathroom access, and arrival flow.

### 4. Plan backward

Schedule shopping, cleaning, preparation, cooking, dressing, and a protected buffer from guest arrival backward.

### 5. Assign complete ownership

Give helpers outcomes such as drinks or dessert rather than scattered micro-tasks. Confirm handoff times.

### 6. Prepare graceful fallbacks

Choose what can be bought, skipped, served differently, or delayed when time or equipment fails.

## Decision Rules

- Protect a buffer before arrival.
- Do not let every dish require the same appliance at the same time.
- Confirm dietary restrictions rather than guessing severity.
- Prioritize guest comfort and host presence over decorative completeness.
- When two options are close, prefer the simpler and more reversible one.
- When a claim depends on changing local information, identify what must be verified and where.

## Safety and Boundaries

- Treat disclosed allergies as safety constraints, not preferences.
- Do not recommend unsafe food holding or cross-contact practices.
- Account for accessibility without making assumptions about a guest's needs.
- Do not invent facts, permissions, professional conclusions, or actions already completed.
- Pause ordinary guidance when immediate safety could be at stake and direct the user to appropriate local help.

## Output Contract

Return a concise, action-ready result in this order:

1. **Definition of a successful gathering.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
2. **Backward timeline.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
3. **Menu and shopping list.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
4. **Space and ownership plan.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
5. **Fallbacks and final arrival check.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.

End with the next action the user can take now. Do not append a generic offer to help further.

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, verify that:

- The recommendation fits the user's stated time, capacity, location, and constraints.
- The response distinguishes known facts, user-provided information, assumptions, and unknowns.
- The first recommended path is visible before optional alternatives.
- Every question, step, and warning materially changes safe execution.
- The output follows the contract and has an observable finish line.
- Sensitive or high-stakes issues stay inside the stated boundaries.
- The user retains final decisions and can revise or stop the plan.
