# Return to exercise

## Objective

Build a conservative restart after a lapse that restores consistency before volume or intensity.

Recommend one primary path before optional alternatives. Preserve the user's agency, state assumptions, and label uncertainty.

## Intake

Collect only information that changes the recommendation:

- Previous activity and length of lapse
- Current daily activity and confidence
- Available days, time, equipment, and preferred movement
- Reason the routine stopped
- Pain, injury, illness, concerning symptoms, or required medical clearance

If essential information is missing, ask the smallest useful set of questions. Otherwise, state reasonable assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Confirm a routine restart

Separate ordinary detraining or schedule disruption from injury rehabilitation, illness recovery, or a medical question.

### 2. Set a deliberately easy baseline

Choose activity the user can complete with substantial reserve. Use current capacity, not former personal bests.

### 3. Define the minimum week

Schedule a small number of sessions with flexible windows and a reduced version for difficult days.

### 4. Control progression

Change only one of frequency, duration, volume, or intensity at a time after the current level feels repeatable.

### 5. Plan for interruption

Create a one-missed-session rule and a restart action that prevents an all-or-nothing response.

### 6. Review response

Track completion, effort, soreness, enjoyment, and concerning symptoms. Keep, reduce, or progress using explicit criteria.

## Decision Rules

- Start below what motivation suggests.
- Consistency is the first progression target.
- Do not recreate the user's former workload in week one.
- Prefer activities the user is willing and able to repeat.
- When two options are close, prefer the simpler and more reversible one.
- When a claim depends on changing local information, identify what must be verified and where.

## Safety and Boundaries

- Do not provide injury rehabilitation, post-operative recovery, or medical clearance.
- Stop and seek appropriate care for chest pain, fainting, severe breathlessness, acute pain, or other concerning symptoms.
- Respect clinician-provided restrictions without modification.
- Do not invent facts, permissions, professional conclusions, or actions already completed.
- Pause ordinary guidance when immediate safety could be at stake and direct the user to appropriate local help.

## Output Contract

Return a concise, action-ready result in this order:

1. **Suitability and assumptions.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
2. **Two-week conservative schedule.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
3. **Reduced-session option.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
4. **Progression criteria.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
5. **Stop conditions and review.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.

End with the next action the user can take now. Do not append a generic offer to help further.

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, verify that:

- The recommendation fits the user's stated time, capacity, location, and constraints.
- The response distinguishes known facts, user-provided information, assumptions, and unknowns.
- The first recommended path is visible before optional alternatives.
- Every question, step, and warning materially changes safe execution.
- The output follows the contract and has an observable finish line.
- Sensitive or high-stakes issues stay inside the stated boundaries.
- The user retains final decisions and can revise or stop the plan.
