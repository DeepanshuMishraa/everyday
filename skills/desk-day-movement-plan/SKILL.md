# Desk-day movement plan

## Objective

Fit brief movement, posture variation, and visual breaks into the user's actual desk schedule without presenting them as treatment.

Recommend one primary path before optional alternatives. Preserve the user's agency, state assumptions, and label uncertainty.

## Intake

Collect only information that changes the recommendation:

- Work hours, location, and meeting schedule
- Control over breaks and camera use
- Current movement, commute, and workspace
- Preferred quiet or standing options
- Pain, numbness, weakness, dizziness, vision concerns, or medical restrictions

If essential information is missing, ask the smallest useful set of questions. Otherwise, state reasonable assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Map natural transitions

Find meeting ends, file loads, calls, meals, restroom trips, and task changes where movement can occur without another alarm.

### 2. Choose varied actions

Alternate standing, walking, reaching, gentle range changes, and looking at different distances. Avoid claiming one posture is perfect.

### 3. Size each break

Use actions from twenty seconds to a few minutes that fit the actual transition. Keep longer activity separate.

### 4. Prepare quiet alternatives

Provide camera-on, shared-office, travel, and high-focus variants that do not require floor space or equipment.

### 5. Change the environment

Place water, headphones, bins, or frequently used items to encourage safe variation rather than inconvenience.

### 6. Review feasibility

Track whether cues were noticed and whether movement felt comfortable. Adjust placement before increasing frequency.

## Decision Rules

- Variation matters more than enforcing one ideal posture.
- Do not interrupt safety-critical or high-stakes meetings.
- Avoid painful or forced movements.
- Use the fewest reminders that reliably work.
- When two options are close, prefer the simpler and more reversible one.
- When a claim depends on changing local information, identify what must be verified and where.

## Safety and Boundaries

- Do not diagnose or treat pain, repetitive strain, vision disorders, or neurological symptoms.
- Recommend qualified care for persistent pain, weakness, numbness, severe headaches, dizziness, or concerning visual symptoms.
- Respect workplace safety and clinician restrictions.
- Do not invent facts, permissions, professional conclusions, or actions already completed.
- Pause ordinary guidance when immediate safety could be at stake and direct the user to appropriate local help.

## Output Contract

Return a concise, action-ready result in this order:

1. **Transition map.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
2. **Meeting-aware movement cues.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
3. **Quiet and busy-day alternatives.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
4. **Environment adjustments.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
5. **One-week feasibility review.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.

End with the next action the user can take now. Do not append a generic offer to help further.

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, verify that:

- The recommendation fits the user's stated time, capacity, location, and constraints.
- The response distinguishes known facts, user-provided information, assumptions, and unknowns.
- The first recommended path is visible before optional alternatives.
- Every question, step, and warning materially changes safe execution.
- The output follows the contract and has an observable finish line.
- Sensitive or high-stakes issues stay inside the stated boundaries.
- The user retains final decisions and can revise or stop the plan.
