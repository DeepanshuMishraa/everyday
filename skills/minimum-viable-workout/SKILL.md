# Minimum viable workout

## Objective

Shorten an intended workout while preserving its most important training effect and a safe stopping rule.

Recommend one primary path before optional alternatives. Preserve the user's agency, state assumptions, and label uncertainty.

## Intake

Collect only information that changes the recommendation:

- Original workout or training goal
- Available minutes including setup
- Energy, experience, and recent training
- Equipment and space
- Pain, injury, illness, concerning symptoms, pregnancy, or medical restrictions

If essential information is missing, ask the smallest useful set of questions. Otherwise, state reasonable assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Screen suitability

Stop ordinary adaptation if the user reports injury, pain, illness, concerning symptoms, or a need for medical clearance.

### 2. Identify the session intent

Name the primary adaptation such as strength practice, aerobic base, technique, or mobility. Preserve only one main intent when time is short.

### 3. Protect the essential work

Keep the most specific movement or intensity block and remove accessories, redundancy, and low-value setup.

### 4. Choose substitutions

Match movement pattern and effort to available equipment and skill. Prefer familiar movements under time pressure.

### 5. Set conservative volume

Specify sets, repetitions or time, rest, and an effort ceiling that leaves technical margin.

### 6. Define stop and finish

Include a short warm-up, pain-free technique checks, stop conditions, and a simple completion record.

## Decision Rules

- Preserve intent before variety.
- Do not compensate for shorter time with reckless intensity.
- Prefer fewer high-quality sets to rushed circuits.
- The user may stop early without turning the session into a failure.
- When two options are close, prefer the simpler and more reversible one.
- When a claim depends on changing local information, identify what must be verified and where.

## Safety and Boundaries

- Do not adapt around acute pain, injury rehabilitation, fainting, chest pain, unusual breathlessness, or other concerning symptoms.
- Do not provide medical clearance or pregnancy-specific programming.
- Encourage qualified coaching for unfamiliar high-risk lifts or equipment.
- Do not invent facts, permissions, professional conclusions, or actions already completed.
- Pause ordinary guidance when immediate safety could be at stake and direct the user to appropriate local help.

## Output Contract

Return a concise, action-ready result in this order:

1. **Session intent.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
2. **Time-boxed warm-up.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
3. **Essential work and substitutions.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
4. **Effort and rest guidance.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
5. **Stop conditions and completion marker.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.

End with the next action the user can take now. Do not append a generic offer to help further.

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, verify that:

- The recommendation fits the user's stated time, capacity, location, and constraints.
- The response distinguishes known facts, user-provided information, assumptions, and unknowns.
- The first recommended path is visible before optional alternatives.
- Every question, step, and warning materially changes safe execution.
- The output follows the contract and has an observable finish line.
- Sensitive or high-stakes issues stay inside the stated boundaries.
- The user retains final decisions and can revise or stop the plan.
