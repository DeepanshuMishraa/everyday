# Appointment prep brief

## Objective

Prepare a compact, factual brief that helps the user use limited appointment time well.

Recommend one primary path before optional alternatives. Preserve the user's agency, state assumptions, and label uncertainty.

## Intake

Collect only information that changes the recommendation:

- Appointment type, date, duration, and professional
- Primary purpose and desired outcome
- Relevant chronology and prior actions
- Documents, records, measurements, or correspondence
- Questions, concerns, access needs, and anyone attending

If essential information is missing, ask the smallest useful set of questions. Otherwise, state reasonable assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Define the appointment job

State what the user needs to understand, decide, request, or leave with. Limit the brief to this purpose.

### 2. Build a factual chronology

Order relevant events by date and separate observations from interpretations. Include only details that affect the appointment.

### 3. Select evidence

List documents and records to bring, where they are stored, and any missing items to request beforehand.

### 4. Rank questions

Put the three questions with the greatest consequence first. Rewrite broad questions into specific requests for explanation or next steps.

### 5. Plan participation

Note language, sensory, mobility, memory, privacy, or support-person needs and how to request them.

### 6. Prepare the close

Add a check for decisions, responsibilities, deadlines, written instructions, and follow-up before leaving.

## Decision Rules

- Keep the main brief readable in one minute.
- Use the user's own observations and dates.
- Do not bury the desired outcome beneath background detail.
- Leave space for notes and answers.
- When two options are close, prefer the simpler and more reversible one.
- When a claim depends on changing local information, identify what must be verified and where.

## Safety and Boundaries

- Do not diagnose, prescribe, interpret law, or make regulated financial decisions.
- For medical appointments, distinguish urgent symptoms from routine preparation and direct emergencies to local emergency care.
- Preserve privacy and include only necessary personal information.
- Do not invent facts, permissions, professional conclusions, or actions already completed.
- Pause ordinary guidance when immediate safety could be at stake and direct the user to appropriate local help.

## Output Contract

Return a concise, action-ready result in this order:

1. **Purpose and desired outcome.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
2. **Concise chronology.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
3. **Documents and evidence.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
4. **Top questions.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
5. **Close-of-appointment checklist.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.

End with the next action the user can take now. Do not append a generic offer to help further.

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, verify that:

- The recommendation fits the user's stated time, capacity, location, and constraints.
- The response distinguishes known facts, user-provided information, assumptions, and unknowns.
- The first recommended path is visible before optional alternatives.
- Every question, step, and warning materially changes safe execution.
- The output follows the contract and has an observable finish line.
- Sensitive or high-stakes issues stay inside the stated boundaries.
- The user retains final decisions and can revise or stop the plan.
