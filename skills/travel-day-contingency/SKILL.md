# Travel-day contingency

## Objective

Audit a travel day for likely failure points and create a compact offline recovery brief.

Recommend one primary path before optional alternatives. Preserve the user's agency, state assumptions, and label uncertainty.

## Intake

Collect only information that changes the recommendation:

- Full dated itinerary with local time zones
- Operators, booking references, airports or stations, and terminals
- Passport, visa, entry, and carrier requirements already verified
- Baggage, medication, mobility, dependents, and connectivity
- Payment methods, insurance, accommodation, and destination transport

If essential information is missing, ask the smallest useful set of questions. Otherwise, state reasonable assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Normalize the timeline

Convert each segment to local date and time, include check-in or boarding cutoffs, and mark independent tickets or terminal changes.

### 2. Audit transfer buffers

Account for immigration, security, baggage reclaim, recheck, walking, transport variability, and the last available onward service.

### 3. Protect critical access

Keep verified documents, medication, keys, payment, contacts, and one change of essentials personally accessible.

### 4. Prepare offline continuity

Save itinerary, addresses, tickets where permitted, maps, policy details, and verified contact routes without relying on one device.

### 5. Plan failure branches

Write the first three actions for delay, cancellation, missed connection, lost bag, dead phone, and closed onward transport.

### 6. Set live verification points

Identify what must be rechecked with authoritative operator or government sources before departure and during travel.

## Decision Rules

- Use local dates and time zones explicitly.
- Do not assume protected connections across separate bookings.
- Independent verified contacts outrank links in unsolicited disruption messages.
- Prioritize safety, documents, medication, shelter, and communication before reimbursement administration.
- When two options are close, prefer the simpler and more reversible one.
- When a claim depends on changing local information, identify what must be verified and where.

## Safety and Boundaries

- Do not guarantee entry, boarding, compensation, or connection feasibility.
- Use current authoritative government and operator sources for border, visa, security, and timetable claims.
- Do not recommend unsafe rushing, bypassing controls, or separating dependents from required support.
- Do not invent facts, permissions, professional conclusions, or actions already completed.
- Pause ordinary guidance when immediate safety could be at stake and direct the user to appropriate local help.

## Output Contract

Return a concise, action-ready result in this order:

1. **Normalized travel timeline.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
2. **Buffer and risk audit.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
3. **Critical-access checklist.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
4. **Failure-branch actions.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
5. **Compact offline fallback brief.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.

End with the next action the user can take now. Do not append a generic offer to help further.

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, verify that:

- The recommendation fits the user's stated time, capacity, location, and constraints.
- The response distinguishes known facts, user-provided information, assumptions, and unknowns.
- The first recommended path is visible before optional alternatives.
- Every question, step, and warning materially changes safe execution.
- The output follows the contract and has an observable finish line.
- Sensitive or high-stakes issues stay inside the stated boundaries.
- The user retains final decisions and can revise or stop the plan.
