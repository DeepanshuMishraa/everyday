## Think like a motion-design team

Separate responsibilities even when one person or agent performs them:

- creative direction protects the idea and taste;
- art direction protects the visual system;
- copy and story protect comprehension;
- animation protects hierarchy and physical logic;
- editing protects time and momentum;
- sound protects impact and continuity;
- compositing protects finish;
- production protects specifications and delivery.

Review each pass through the relevant role instead of trying to solve every concern simultaneously.

## Build the cue sheet

Map one shared timeline with:

- scene and story beat;
- narration clause or on-screen copy;
- musical phrase, downbeat, fill, and stop;
- primary visual action;
- secondary accents;
- transition overlap;
- sound effect;
- readable hold;
- acceptance test.

Use frames for implementation and seconds for human review. Derive frame counts from the approved frame rate; never bake assumptions about 24, 30, or 60fps into the story.

## Create motion grammar

Define a small vocabulary:

- two or three entrance families;
- one default movement ease and one impact ease;
- one primary transition family;
- one accent transition;
- rules for scale, blur, overshoot, rotation, and parallax;
- rules for cursor, terminal, browser, device, and type motion.

Tie the vocabulary to the brand. A precise technical product may snap, align, and resolve. An expressive creative product may stretch, smear, collide, and rebound. Consistency should feel authored, not repetitive.

## Choreograph scenes

Animate in hierarchy order:

1. camera or scene-level move;
2. dominant product or headline;
3. proof action;
4. supporting labels and structure;
5. texture and ambient motion.

Use anticipation, action, settle, and hold. Give weight through distance, duration, easing, blur, scale, and sound—not gratuitous bounce. Offset related elements by a coherent rhythm. Let important actions complete before asking the viewer to read.

Build contrast across the film:

- dense motion followed by stillness;
- wide spatial move followed by precise microinteraction;
- light frame followed by structural dark;
- restrained proof followed by a decisive payoff;
- fast transition followed by a readable hold.

## Choreograph transitions

Transitions must connect ideas, direction, shape, color, or sound. Use match cuts, shared edges, object handoffs, masks, camera moves, or product-state continuity.

Start the incoming scene before the outgoing scene fully leaves. Preserve a focal anchor so the eye knows where to land. Reserve the most elaborate transition for a genuine narrative escalation.

## Composite for finish

Agency-grade finish comes from accumulated control:

- clean masks and edge treatment;
- consistent motion blur;
- believable depth and occlusion;
- restrained shadows and highlights;
- color continuity across product captures;
- subtle grain or texture applied as a system;
- optical alignment, not only mathematical centering;
- no accidental browser artifacts, low-resolution captures, or mismatched corner radii.

Use precompositions or wrappers to separate entrance, ambient, and camera motion. Keep effects editable and deterministic. Prefer a few excellent treatments over a pile of plug-in effects.

## Polish in passes

Review in this order:

1. **Story pass** — understandable without polish.
2. **Blocking pass** — composition, scale, and timing.
3. **Animation pass** — hierarchy, easing, and physical logic.
4. **Transition pass** — continuity between scenes.
5. **Type pass** — line breaks, tracking, optical alignment, readability.
6. **Composite pass** — masks, depth, texture, color, and cleanup.
7. **Sound pass** — sync, hierarchy, and mix.
8. **Delivery pass** — full-speed playback on target devices and exact exports.

At each pass, fix the largest perceptual problem first. Do not polish a scene whose story function is still unclear.
