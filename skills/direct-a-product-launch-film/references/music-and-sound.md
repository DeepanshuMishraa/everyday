## Start from the edit

Define the score's job before searching:

- target duration and whether the ending must resolve or loop;
- emotional trajectory;
- energy curve by story beat;
- narration and frequency-space requirements;
- approximate tempo range;
- desired instrumentation and prohibited clichés;
- delivery platform and loudness constraints.

Write direction in musical and editorial terms. “Confident electronic pulse, 118–124 BPM, restrained opening, clear lift for the reveal, half-time proof section, decisive final hit” is more actionable than “epic tech music.”

## Source or generate music

Use one of four paths:

1. **Licensed library** — fastest when the user has access. Verify the license covers the intended platforms, paid media, territories, term, and client use.
2. **Commissioned composer** — best for a distinctive sonic identity and precise picture sync. Supply the brief, rough cut, hit points, stems, and revision expectations.
3. **Generative provider** — useful for bespoke structure when its terms permit commercial publication. Record provider, model or preset, prompt, generation date, and license.
4. **Original procedural score** — useful for reliable, editable rhythm beds and UI-led motion. Avoid generic placeholder loops in the final.

Never treat “downloadable” as “licensed.” Do not imitate a living artist or recognizable copyrighted track. Preserve receipts, license text, attribution requirements, and source links in `audio_meta.json`.

If browsing or a media provider is available, search by editorial need: duration, BPM, structure, instrumentation, mood, and ending. Audition against the storyboard or animatic, not in isolation.

## Select a track

Prefer music with:

- an identifiable pulse without requiring every motion to hit every beat;
- phrase boundaries that match scene-scale changes;
- useful fills, risers, drops, stops, and a clean final cadence;
- contrast between sections;
- enough headroom for voice and sound effects;
- stems or an instrumental version when possible.

Reject tracks that take too long to develop, lack a usable ending, force the wrong tone, or become fatiguing under repeated review.

## Build a beat map

Analyze the final audio file, not a streaming preview. Record:

- sample rate and exact duration;
- tempo and meter;
- beat and downbeat timestamps;
- phrase boundaries;
- intensity by section;
- fills, pickups, drops, stops, and final hit;
- edit points that tolerate shortening or extension.

Store the result in a machine-readable beat map and summarize the editorial landmarks in the storyboard. Correct automatic beat detection by ear.

## Edit and arrange

Edit on musical phrases when possible. Cut or extend using repeated harmonic sections, stems, or clean room-tone tails. Do not hard-loop a track across a vocal pickup or unresolved chord.

For a custom-length film:

1. secure the opening identity;
2. preserve the reveal lift;
3. allocate a stable groove to proof;
4. reserve the largest change for payoff or CTA;
5. create a deliberate ending rather than fading because time ran out.

Use stems to thin the arrangement beneath narration, rebuild transitions, and make the climax larger through contrast.

## Design sound

Use sound effects to explain contact, scale, speed, material, or state:

- soft tick for selection;
- transient plus low body for a reveal;
- short tonal confirmation for success;
- controlled whoosh for directional continuity;
- texture for mechanical or tactile motion.

Layer sparingly. Match the visual material and perspective. Pitch, trim, filter, and envelope sounds into one family. Avoid stock “click,” “swoosh,” and cinematic boom sounds that advertise their source.

## Mix and verify

Establish hierarchy: narration, meaningful product sounds, music, decorative effects. Automate music around speech instead of lowering the entire track. Remove low-frequency buildup and harsh repeated transients.

Monitor on headphones, laptop speakers, and a phone. Check mono compatibility and the platform's current loudness requirements. Prevent clipping and leave sensible true-peak headroom. Watch the whole film with sound, muted, and audio-only before approval.
