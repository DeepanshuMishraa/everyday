## Brand translation

Keep brand atoms exact: colors, type families, casing, corner language, line weight, icon style, and tone. Adapt their application for video.

Define one canvas, one ink, one accent, and one structural dark. Additional colors require a functional role such as success or syntax. Scarcity gives the accent power.

Write explicit bans. Typical bans include generic neon gradients, glow-heavy dark SaaS styling, interchangeable card grids, pure black/white when the brand uses tinted neutrals, and unrelated stock imagery.

## Frame composition

Build three layers:

1. **Background** — grid, grain, ghost type, localized color field, rules, or spatial texture.
2. **Midground** — the product message and primary surface.
3. **Foreground** — index labels, progress bars, crop marks, status, cursor, or registration details.

Use two focal points so the eye travels. Anchor to edges and zones. A centered stack is justified only for a deliberate impact beat.

At full build, target eight to ten meaningful elements. Density should create life, not equal weighting.

## Typography

- Headlines: roughly 64–180px at 1080p depending on length.
- Body: roughly 28–42px.
- Labels: roughly 18–24px.
- Commands: large enough to read on a phone-feed preview.

Use display type for emotion, body type for explanation, and mono for technical or indexical truth. Never shrink essential copy to preserve a weak layout; redesign the frame.

## Product surfaces

Screenshots are raw material, not finished shots. Apply at least one:

- crop to the decisive region;
- controlled pan or scroll;
- browser, device, or terminal framing;
- layered depth or parallax;
- focus change;
- extracted UI state animated separately.

Avoid faded full-page screenshots behind large text. They create visual mud and rarely prove anything.

Reconstruct only enough UI to show the real interaction. Preserve product truth and labels.

## Motion language

Give each action a verb:

- **slam** — kinetic phrase or product reveal;
- **streak** — fast card or word arrival;
- **push** — one state physically displaces another;
- **assemble** — fragments form a product or result;
- **select** — cursor, press, selected state, progress;
- **resolve** — technical proof becomes brand promise or CTA.

Use transform aliases for spatial motion. Entrances use out-eases; exits use in-eases; moves between positions use in-out eases.

Vary:

- direction;
- duration;
- ease family;
- scale versus translation;
- stagger rhythm;
- stillness after motion.

Do not stack unrelated transform tweens on the same element. Split entrance and ambient motion across parent and child wrappers.

## Music synchronization

Create one beat map. Record:

- tempo;
- beat and downbeat timestamps;
- fills and rolls;
- high/low energy phases;
- surges;
- hard stops.

Use downbeats for scene changes and major reveals. Use secondary hits for words, clicks, status changes, and progress. Let low-energy phases hold readable information.

The visual energy curve should follow the music’s structure, not every drum hit. Constant hitting becomes flat.

## Transition system

Choose:

- one primary transition for 60–70% of cuts;
- one accent transition for topic changes;
- one climax treatment.

For high-energy editorial launches, reliable families include zoom-through, directional push, squeeze, fast blur, overexposure, and staggered cover. Keep transitions around 0.15–0.3 seconds unless the music calls for a longer breath.

Outgoing and incoming scenes must move concurrently. Do not fade one out, leave a gap, then start the next.

## Product reveal recipe

1. Start with a kinetic or structural precursor.
2. Hit a full-saturation brand moment.
3. Assemble or slam the product identity.
4. Add a proof fragment or category deck.
5. Land the promise and hold.

Avoid a centered wordmark floating over a faded screenshot.

## Interaction-proof recipe

1. Establish the inferior state.
2. Introduce the new UI as the dominant surface.
3. Move a cursor or focus indicator to the target.
4. Press/select with tactile compression.
5. Change selected state.
6. Advance progress or show confirmation.
7. Hold long enough to understand the consequence.

The action and result must be visible in the same shot.

## Closing-action recipe

1. Complete the technical proof: receipt, success, output, or live state.
2. Reveal a human-readable promise.
3. Present the URL, command, QR code, or CTA at dominant scale.
4. Use one final tactile hit.
5. Hold the complete frame.

Dark structural closers work well when the preceding film is light, provided the brand supports the dark surface. Keep the CTA and URL high-contrast.
