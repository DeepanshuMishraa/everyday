# Scripting and Voice

## Choose the script mode

Choose intentionally:

- **No narration** — best when product interaction, typography, and music can carry the argument.
- **Narration-led** — best when the value needs context, abstraction, or emotional continuity.
- **Founder or customer voice** — best when credibility and point of view matter more than polish.
- **Dialogue or testimonial** — best when a real human tension and outcome are available.

Do not add narration merely to explain visuals that already communicate. Do not force a silent film when the product needs conceptual framing.

## Build the argument

Write these lines before writing prose:

- audience tension;
- product promise;
- visible proof;
- resulting change;
- next action.

Turn them into a spoken argument with one causal chain. Use product truth, not category clichés. A launch script should sound like a confident person showing something valuable, not a press release.

## Write narration

Estimate timing from an appropriate delivery speed:

- 120–140 words per minute for deliberate or technical explanation;
- 140–165 for conversational launch narration;
- 165–185 only for intentionally energetic copy with simple language.

These ranges are starting points. Time a real read and leave room for emphasis, breathing, product sounds, and visual comprehension.

Use short clauses, concrete verbs, and pronounceable language. Vary sentence length. Put important words near visual events. Give each line one job. Read every draft aloud.

Avoid:

- opening with company history;
- repeated “we believe” framing;
- unsupported superlatives;
- feature lists without consequence;
- jargon that cannot be visualized;
- narrating every click.

Format the script in two columns or aligned blocks:

| Time | Voice / picture |
|---|---|
| `00:00–00:04` | Spoken line / exact visual proof |

Include pronunciation notes, performance intent, pauses, and alternate trims.

## Write on-screen copy

Narration and typography should share the work. Do not duplicate full spoken sentences on screen. Use typography for the phrase that must survive muted playback: problem, promise, proof label, result, URL, or CTA.

Keep one dominant thought per frame. Prefer verbs and outcomes. Preserve exact product names, commands, URLs, and metrics. Test copy at feed size and for the shortest intended cutdown.

## Direct the voice

Specify character, pace, intimacy, energy, and prohibited mannerisms. For example: “Warm, precise, slightly amused; speak to one informed viewer; no trailer voice, no sales cadence.”

Use a human performer or a synthetic voice only with appropriate consent and publication rights. Do not clone or imitate an identifiable person's voice without authorization. Capture clean room tone and multiple full takes before line pickups.

## Time and revise

Create a scratch read early and cut the animatic to it. Then revise words against picture:

1. remove lines the visual already proves;
2. replace abstract claims with observable consequences;
3. shorten clauses that collide with transitions;
4. protect silence around the reveal and final action;
5. re-record after the picture structure stabilizes.

The final test is comprehension after one watch, not whether every approved phrase survived.
