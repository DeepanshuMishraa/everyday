# Packing by scenario

## Objective

Pack for the actual itinerary and consequences of failure while staying within luggage constraints.

Recommend one primary path before optional alternatives. Preserve the user's agency, state assumptions, and label uncertainty.

## Intake

Collect only information that changes the recommendation:

- Destinations, dates, itinerary, and current weather source
- Activities, formality, local norms, and access needs
- Luggage dimensions, weight, and carrier rules
- Laundry, accommodation, and shopping access
- Documents, medications, devices, and hard-to-replace essentials

If essential information is missing, ask the smallest useful set of questions. Otherwise, state reasonable assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Map scenarios

List transit, sleep, routine days, weather exposure, work, exercise, events, and return travel. Remove imagined scenarios with no itinerary support.

### 2. Set critical items

Identify documents, prescribed medication, accessibility items, keys, payment access, and devices whose absence has serious consequences.

### 3. Build a clothing system

Choose repeatable layers and compatible items by scenario, weather, laundry, and local norms rather than by day alone.

### 4. Allocate luggage

Place critical and first-day items in the personally carried bag. Check liquid, battery, sharp, and restricted-item rules from current authoritative sources.

### 5. Cut and verify

Remove duplicates with low consequence, weigh or measure the bag, and preserve a small capacity buffer.

### 6. Run the departure sweep

Check documents, medication, chargers, keys, wallet, itinerary, home shutdown, and the wear-in-transit plan.

## Decision Rules

- Consequences of forgetting outrank convenience.
- Do not pack for every remote possibility.
- Verify current weather and operator rules; do not rely on memory.
- Keep critical items accessible and distributed when practical.
- When two options are close, prefer the simpler and more reversible one.
- When a claim depends on changing local information, identify what must be verified and where.

## Safety and Boundaries

- Do not advise carrying prohibited, undeclared, or improperly stored items.
- Do not alter prescribed medication or documentation requirements.
- Ask for destination and nationality context before discussing border or cultural requirements.
- Do not invent facts, permissions, professional conclusions, or actions already completed.
- Pause ordinary guidance when immediate safety could be at stake and direct the user to appropriate local help.

## Output Contract

Return a concise, action-ready result in this order:

1. **Scenario map.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
2. **Critical carry-on list.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
3. **Clothing and gear system.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
4. **Cut list and constraint check.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
5. **Final departure sweep.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.

End with the next action the user can take now. Do not append a generic offer to help further.

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, verify that:

- The recommendation fits the user's stated time, capacity, location, and constraints.
- The response distinguishes known facts, user-provided information, assumptions, and unknowns.
- The first recommended path is visible before optional alternatives.
- Every question, step, and warning materially changes safe execution.
- The output follows the contract and has an observable finish line.
- Sensitive or high-stakes issues stay inside the stated boundaries.
- The user retains final decisions and can revise or stop the plan.
