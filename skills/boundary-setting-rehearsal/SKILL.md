# Boundary-setting rehearsal

## Objective

Define and rehearse a specific boundary that the user can communicate and consistently uphold.

Recommend one primary path before optional alternatives. Preserve the user's agency, state assumptions, and label uncertainty.

## Intake

Collect only information that changes the recommendation:

- Relationship and recurring situation
- Behavior the user wants to limit or change
- What remains acceptable
- Likely pushback and practical dependencies
- Safety, power imbalance, and preferred communication channel

If essential information is missing, ask the smallest useful set of questions. Otherwise, state reasonable assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Define the boundary

Write the user's own action or limit, not a rule intended to control another person. Make the triggering situation observable.

### 2. Choose the minimum explanation

Give enough context to be clear without building a case for permission. Remove accusations, diagnoses, and debate invitations.

### 3. State the follow-through

Name what the user will do if the situation recurs. Ensure it is safe, proportionate, and within the user's control.

### 4. Rehearse predictable responses

Practice replies to guilt, bargaining, anger, minimization, repeated questions, and apparent agreement without change.

### 5. Plan delivery

Choose timing, medium, privacy, and support that improve safety and clarity. Avoid delivering during peak conflict when possible.

### 6. Review consistency

Set a point to assess whether the boundary is clear, workable, and being followed by the user.

## Decision Rules

- A boundary describes what the user will do.
- Do not over-explain after the limit has been understood.
- Use a broken-record response when repeated argument is the main pressure.
- Adjust the boundary if the user cannot safely or realistically follow through.
- When two options are close, prefer the simpler and more reversible one.
- When a claim depends on changing local information, identify what must be verified and where.

## Safety and Boundaries

- Do not use ordinary communication rehearsal for coercive control, stalking, abuse, or immediate danger.
- Prioritize a trusted person, specialist service, or local emergency help when safety is uncertain.
- Do not pressure disclosure, confrontation, reconciliation, or continued contact.
- Do not invent facts, permissions, professional conclusions, or actions already completed.
- Pause ordinary guidance when immediate safety could be at stake and direct the user to appropriate local help.

## Output Contract

Return a concise, action-ready result in this order:

1. **Boundary definition.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
2. **Short primary script.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
3. **Follow-through action.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
4. **Pushback rehearsal.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
5. **Delivery and review plan.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.

End with the next action the user can take now. Do not append a generic offer to help further.

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, verify that:

- The recommendation fits the user's stated time, capacity, location, and constraints.
- The response distinguishes known facts, user-provided information, assumptions, and unknowns.
- The first recommended path is visible before optional alternatives.
- Every question, step, and warning materially changes safe execution.
- The output follows the contract and has an observable finish line.
- Sensitive or high-stakes issues stay inside the stated boundaries.
- The user retains final decisions and can revise or stop the plan.
