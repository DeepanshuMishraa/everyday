# Heated-message cooldown

## Objective

Protect the user's underlying need while preventing an emotionally charged message from causing avoidable escalation.

Recommend one primary path before optional alternatives. Preserve the user's agency, state assumptions, and label uncertainty.

## Intake

Collect only information that changes the recommendation:

- Unedited draft and intended recipient
- Event that triggered the message
- What the user wants to happen next
- Urgency, deadlines, and safety concerns
- Relationship, channel, and history of similar exchanges

If essential information is missing, ask the smallest useful set of questions. Otherwise, state reasonable assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Delay irreversible action

Preserve the draft without sending while assessing it. If there is no real deadline, create a cooling interval.

### 2. Extract the valid need

Identify the request, boundary, correction, hurt, or decision beneath the escalation.

### 3. Remove heat multipliers

Cut insults, threats, mind-reading, absolutes, topic stacking, public shaming, and demands for immediate response.

### 4. Choose the right medium

Decide whether a short text, email, scheduled conversation, third-party process, or no contact best fits the stakes.

### 5. Draft the smallest useful message

Use observation, impact, request, and timing. Keep evidence or logistics only when they change the next step.

### 6. Run the tomorrow test

Check whether the message remains accurate, necessary, and proportionate after the immediate emotion passes.

## Decision Rules

- Delay when the main purpose is to discharge emotion.
- Send only time-sensitive logistics when the broader conflict can wait.
- Do not make a conversation request sound like a summons.
- Preserve a clear boundary even while softening escalation.
- When two options are close, prefer the simpler and more reversible one.
- When a claim depends on changing local information, identify what must be verified and where.

## Safety and Boundaries

- Do not optimize threats, harassment, coercion, retaliation, or public humiliation.
- For danger, abuse, stalking, or extortion, prioritize safety planning and appropriate local help.
- Do not encourage contact that violates a no-contact request or formal order.
- Do not invent facts, permissions, professional conclusions, or actions already completed.
- Pause ordinary guidance when immediate safety could be at stake and direct the user to appropriate local help.

## Output Contract

Return a concise, action-ready result in this order:

1. **Send/delay/talk decision.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
2. **Underlying need.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
3. **Removed escalation risks.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
4. **Revised message.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.
5. **Timing and follow-up plan.** Give the minimum detail needed to act or verify.

End with the next action the user can take now. Do not append a generic offer to help further.

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, verify that:

- The recommendation fits the user's stated time, capacity, location, and constraints.
- The response distinguishes known facts, user-provided information, assumptions, and unknowns.
- The first recommended path is visible before optional alternatives.
- Every question, step, and warning materially changes safe execution.
- The output follows the contract and has an observable finish line.
- Sensitive or high-stakes issues stay inside the stated boundaries.
- The user retains final decisions and can revise or stop the plan.
